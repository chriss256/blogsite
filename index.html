Race Against Rust:
How Reaction Rates Impact Metal Decay

Ever Wonder Why Your Bike Chain Rusts Faster After a Rainy Day?

If you have ever left your bike outside in the rain, you probably have noticed that it starts to rust. This is due to a chemical reaction that eats away at metal over time called corrosion. However, corrosion doesn’t always occur at the same speed its speed, called reaction rate, depends on the environment it is exposed to. Understanding reaction rate can help us to protect everything from bikes to bridges, structures, and machinery.

What is Corrosion

There are many different forms of corrosion, such as uniform corrosion, pitting, crevice corrosion, galvanic corrosion, stress-corrosion cracking, and more, but the most common is rust, a type of electrochemical corrosion where iron reacts with oxygen and water. Corrosion is the blanket term for chemical and electrochemical reactions that break down metals. In rusting, iron atoms lose their electrons (Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2 e⁻) at anodic sites while oxygen gains electrons at cathodic sites, producing flaky iron oxides (Fe₂O₃·nH₂O), rust, that peels away to expose fresh metal and repeat the cycle.

Measuring Corrosion Rates

Units of Measurement

Corrosion is measured by how much metal thickness is lost per year, in micrometers per year (µm/yr) or mils per year (mpy).

Faraday's Law

Faraday’s Law states that mass loss (Δm) relates to charge (I·t) in

Δm = (I × t × M) / (n × F)

Where M is the metal’s mass, n is electrons transferred, and F is Faraday’s constant

Polarization Curves

Plotting current vs. potential, scientists found the corrosion potential (E₍ₒᵣᵣ₎) and corrosion current density (I₍ₒᵣᵣ₎), which directly translate into a rate of metal lost

Factors That Impact Corrosion Rates

Temperature

Arrhenius relationship: Reaction rates roughly double for every 10 °C increase, meaning hot environments can quickly speed up corrosion rates.

pH / Acidity

For many steels and alloys, corrosion rates are lowest in near-neutral conditions and rise in acidic conditions.

Electrolyte Composition

Chloride ions (electrolytes) commonly break down protective oxide films, forming tiny sections that can quickly grow, allowing corrosion can go from invisible to severe in just a few days.

Galvanic Coupling

When two dissimilar metals are electrically connected in an electrolyte, the more unstable metal becomes the anode and corrodes faster. The greater the difference and smaller the anode’s area relative to the cathode, the quicker the corrosion occurs.

Controlling Corrosion Rates

Cathodic Protection

Cathodic Protection involves creating a small protective current or attaching sacrificial anodes. This can lower corrosion rates from hundreds of µm/yr down to under 10 µm/yr.

Barrier Coatings

Paints, epoxies, and galvanizing to physically isolate metal from moisture and ions, coatings can delay maintenance by 50–150 years.

Corrosion Inhibitors

Corrosion inhibitors can be added in small doses to water or process fluids, inhibitors form thin films that stop oxidation. Common types include anodic passivators and mixed inhibitors. They’re important for cooling systems, pipelines, and storage tanks.

Conclusion

In conclusion, understanding reaction rates in corrosion is important, from preventing the rust on your bike to saving multi-million-dollar structures from collapse. Understanding how temperature, acidity, and electrolyte composition affect the rate of reaction can help people to design better anodic protection, barrier coatings, and inhibitors that win the “race” against rust.
